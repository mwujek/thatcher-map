People

Doa Rosa (Rosa Real Mateo de Nieto)

1900-1980

Doa Rosa is one of Mexican folk arts most esteemed artists, known for her revitalization of the ancient Zapotec technique of barro negro ceramics. In the 1950s, Doa Rosa began burnishing unfired clay using a quartz crystal that created a lacquer-like, silvery gleen in the black glaze. This single discovery, a process that causes a molecular change, transformed the ancient barro negro tradition into a new art form. The entire process for each object takes twenty to thirty days. The clay is gathered from the land near her home. Each vessel is built in layers and allowed to air dry before adding more coils. The final firing takes eight hours. Her family continues the tradition in her workshop in San Bartolo Coyotepec, Oaxaca. Her work is featured at the entrance of the exhition.

Manuel Jimnez Ramrez (Don Manuel)

1919-2005

Manuel Jimnez Ramrez, Don Manuel, was a Oaxacan wood carver who developed his own version of the alebrije in his chosen medium. After experimenting with dozens of wood types, Don Manuel began using copalillo (or copal) exclusively which has been adopted by the majority of carvers in the region. He was one of the first to commercialize wood carving, introducing the industry to entire villages in Oaxaca. In addition to wooden alebrijes, Don Manuel was also known for his carved animals, human figurines, masks, and religious/nativity scenes. The rabbit in the central area of the gallery is attributed to him.

Teodora Blanco

1928-1980

Teodora Blanco was born in Santa Mara de Atzompa, Oaxaca. Her signature pieces include fantastical and human-like figures bearing animal heads or horns, as well as frogs and nursing children formed with unglazed clay. Most notable are her imaginative hollow ceramic women known as muecas that are variations on the large water cooling vessels called tinajeras. Blanco is also widely known for pioneering a type of decoration called pastillaje, a form of clay applique that is used to resemble embroidery on traditional terra cotta pottery, most widely seen in the clothing of her figurines. Her work caught the attention of Nelson A. Rockefeller, who collected more than 175 of her works when they met in 1978. He said of Blanco: She is a person of such an active mind, so feeling and creative, that one thought leads to another and each is expressed in sculptural form through some additions to or elaboration of the piece in hand with lighting speed and sensitivity. Today, Blancos workshop continues in the hands of her family.

Josefina Aguilar

1950-

Josefina Aguilar, along with her three sisters, has built her life and legacy in Ocatlan, Oaxaca, where she still creates art and resides with her eleven children, most of whom are artists who help in her workshop. She specializes in muecas, human figures made of clay, and considers the face of each piece to be her signature. It's my way of putting a little bit of myself into each piece. Each time I create a piece I try to make it different in some way from any other piece I've made, but i like to have the face resemble me and the women of this area so it will remind the person seeing it of the author.

Pedro Linares

1906-1992

Pedro Linares is widely revered for his fantastical papier mach creatures called alebrijes. Linares imagined his first colorful monster during a feverish hallucination after falling ill around 1940. alebrijes quickly gained popularity and were shown in many galleries around Mexico City. This eventually caught the eye of Diego Rivera and Frida Kahlo, who both began commissioning the creatures from Linares. While many artists continue to create alebrijes today, the Linares family members (Enrique, Felipe, Miguel, Leonardo, David, and Ricardo) are known as the masters of the authentic alebrijes. In 1990, the Mexican Government awarded Pedro Linares the National Prize for Popular Arts and Traditions (Premio Nacional de Ciencias y Artes en la Rama VI, Artes y Tradiciones Populares). The alebrije on display in Dobles Vidas is from the Linares workshop.

Candelario Medrano

1918-1986

Candelario Medrano was the adopted son of Julian Acero, the top ceramic toy maker in his village of Santa Cruz de las Huertas, Jalisco. Medrano worked most of his life constructing clay sewer pipes while creating art on the side. It wasnt until later in life that he focused completely on his craft. Today, he is best known for his large nagual figures, mythical animals with human faces that are used for protection against evil as well as miniature models, many of crowded buses and densely populated apartment buildings complete with furnishings and inhabitants. Even after gaining international fame, Medrano continued living and working in his original studio where chickens and grandchildren were given free reign. Dobles Vidas shows two of his pieces, including a UFO at the end of the Traditions hallway.

Places

Oaxaca

The state of Oaxaca is famous for its folk art. The region is home to famed artists such as Doa Rosa (San Bartolo Coyotepec), Manuel Jimnez Ramrez and Family (San Antonio Arrazola), and Josefina Aguilar (Ocotln), all of whom are represented in Dobles Vidas. Nelson Rockefeller frequently visited the region in search of folk art and befriended many of the artists.

Atzompa, Oaxaca

The city of Atzompa is known for its pottery making, and particularly for being the hometown of Teodora Blanco and family. The clay used by the artists is mined from San Lorenzo Cacautepec, less than three miles from the town center. Atzompa is located near the ancient Zapotec city of Monte Albn, the place where barro negro pottery was first created. Doa Rosa later revived the artform in her hometown of San Bartolo Coyotepec.

Guanajuato

Guanajuato is best known for its masks, both papier mach and ceramic, and for developing the technique for firing ceramics at high temperatures. In Dobles Vidas, the monkey mask and mold come from this region. Guanajuato is also the birthplace of acclaimed artist Diego Rivera.

Michoacn

Michoacn is famous for a variety of media, especially glazed ceramic plates, bowls, vessels, and painted muecas such as Juan Diego Addressing the Virgen de Guadalupe by Candelario Medrano (in the case to your right). Hand-formed copper and woven items such as baskets, floor coverings, dolls and feathered work are also popular in the state.

Puebla

Less traveled, Puebla is visited mainly by Mexican folk art collectors interested in its prolific ceramics tradition and its distinct Tree of Life sculptures. Artists in rural areas also create a wide variety of works related to traditional celebrations and holidays such as the Day of the Dead.

Chiapas

Chiapas is home to one of the largest indigenous populations in Mexico; Mayan descendants have lived in the region for at least one thousand years. These artists are responsible for producing the majority of handcrafts and folk art in the state and specialize in weaving and ancient textile designs. These textiles are woven for daily use as well as used in storytelling and sacred rituals. Dobles Vidas presents several Chiapas hats that are worn exclusively by men during ceremonies and resemble the glorious headdresses worn by Mayan ancestors.

Jalisco and Nayarit

The Huichol, a deeply spiritual indigenous people living in the mountains of Jalisco and Nayarit, produce a variety of folk art and are most famous for their bead work and hand woven yarn paintings called tablas. Tablas were originally made by shamans to represent visions and given as offerings to the gods. The sacred tradition has since become a commercialized craft and making tablas is a sustainable source of income for indigenous artists in urban areas.

Tonal, Jalisco

Jalisco is one of the most significant pottery centers in Mexico. The city of Tonal is known for its unique two-step ceramic process that involves baking the clay twice before polishing its surface with stones so the piece appears glazed.

Metepec, Estado de México

Within the State of Mexico, the city of Metepec is famous for its elaborate Trees of Life and Trees of Death candelabras. The clay sculptures are made from molds and then refined by hand. Traditionally these objects were used by Catholic missionaries to explain Biblical stories during colonial times; now they are used as decoration.

Guerrero

Onal, Guerrero is the hub of lacquerware in Mexico.

Traditions

Alebrijes

The alebrije was invented by Pedro Linares (1906-1992) after he imagined the creatures in a fever-induced hallucination. The colorful, papier mach dragons quickly became famous and were shown in many art galleries throughout Mexico. While many artists started to imitate the alebrijes the Linares family remains the masters.

  • States: Oaxaca & State of Mexico
  • People: Linares & Ramirez

Barro Negro

Barro negro (black clay) is created by burnishing air-dried clay with quartz before firing. At a critical point during firing, the artist seals all openings to the kiln and adds fuel to the fire, causing the clay to absorb the black carbon soot. Doa Rosa is credited with reviving the ancient Zapotec craft.

  • States: Oaxaca
  • People: Dona Rosa

Ceramic Finishes

Ceramics in Mexico are finished in a number of ways. In this exhibition, you will find bare fired clay, burnished (rubbed) clay, painted and slip-painted (paint with thin clay added) clay, brown, green and white glazes and barro negro.

  • States: Oaxaca, Michoacan & Puebla

Hats

Traditional costumes are worn during festivals throughout Mexico. Dances are an essential feature of most festivals and the villagers who participate take their roles seriously. The necessary regalia, which can be extremely costly, is made by local craftsmen and paid for by performers and their families. In the Chiapas highlands the status of native officials is shown by special items of ceremonial dress. Worn exclusively by men, the hats resemble headdresses worn by Mayan ancestors.

  • State: Chiapas

Huipil

A pre-Hispanic embroidered garment, the huipil, is widely worn by women in Mexico and is often a symbol of status for the weaver.

  • State: Oaxaca

Lacquerware

Mexican lacquerware is made with rayado, a carving technique practiced in Guerrero using layers of different colored lacquer. The top layer is etched away to create intricate flowers, animals, and geometric designs. Olinal, Guerrero is the hub of the craft in Mexico.

  • State: Guerrero

Masks

Ceramic molds are used to make papier mach masks sold in Mexican markets for parties and holidays. The masks are made by pressing two or three layers of the papier mach onto the mold. When dry, the mask lifts off the mold ready to be painted. Dobles Vidas also has several wooden masks on display.

  • State: Guanajuato

Muecas

Literally translated as dolls, these human figurines are shaped from clay by hand. Artists such as Josefina Aguilar (1950-) and Candelario Medrano (1918-1986) paint and glaze them into Mexican icons such as Frida Kahlo and the Virgin of Guadalupe; while Teodora Blancos muecas were made of unglazed clay and feature whimsical human-like animals.

  • State: Oaxaca
  • People: Aguilar, Medrano, Blanco

Tablas

The Huichol people are known for the imagination and skill expressed in their yarn tablas, or yarn paintings. The artist soften and spread wax over a wooden board. A sharp instrument is used scratch in the designs and then press the brightly colored strands of wool onto the surface. The paintings often depict rituals, myths or visions from pagan gods.

  • States: Jalisco & Nayarit

Flor de Tonal

Named for its city of origin in the state of Jalisco, Tonal pottery is recognized by its burnished (polished with stone to a flat gloss) and slip-painted (paint enhanced with thin clay) animal and floral motifs. The finely delineated leaves and flowers, called Flor de Tonal, reflect Spanish influences.

  • State: Jalisco

"Tree of Life" and "Tree of Death"

c

Adorned with flowers and animals, the "Tree of Life" symbolizes fertility and rebirth. Introduced to Mexico with the Spanish conquest, they became particularly popular with potters throughout Puebla and in Metepec, State of Mexico. The candle-bearing sculptures typically commemorate the divine creation before the fall of Adam and Eve. The "Tree of Death" is often depicted with tree branches and decorated with grinning skeletons.

  • States: Puebla & State of Mexico

Wood carving

Manuel Jimnez Ramrez (1919-2005) was one of the first to commercialize wood carving and identified copalillo (copal) as the best type of wood t be used. These intricately painted wood carvings are often of animals and alebrijes.

  • State: Oaxaca
